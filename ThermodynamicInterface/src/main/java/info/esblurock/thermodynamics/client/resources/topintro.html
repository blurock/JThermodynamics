The Benson additivity method1, including its symmetry and steric corrections, gives an accurate estimation of the thermodynamic properties, Cp (at several temperatures), ΔH298 and ΔS298 of an arbitrary Lewis structure and is essential for automatic mechanism generation systems. THERGAS2 and now its flexible and extensible descendent, JTHERGAS3, use an extensive database of individual atom bonding (Benson) structures for a first order estimation. Second order corrections are made with stearic hindrance, ring strain and internal and external symmetry contributions. Radical thermodynamics are computed as a disassociation reaction of the parent taking into account symmetry and vibrational differences between the parent and radical and a disassociation energy estimation. The significant design improvement of JTHERGAS is the extensible and easily manageable database of structures and corrections and the multiple interfaces to both the calculator and database. Extensibility is essential for computing the thermodynamics of new classes of species with new structural features such as in, for example, new fuels and biofuels.
